I Ran My First 5k!

May 15, 2026

On Saturday, May 9th, I successfully ran my first ever 5k. In fact, that was my first race ever in general. In fact, that was the first time I had ever run more than 2 miles continuously. Before I started training for this race, I had never run more than a mile, and that was 8 years ago in high school. Running this race was by far the hardest thing that I have ever done regarding physical fitness, and it sucked. I started training for this race almost 3 months ago, and I would not consider myself consistent. My goal was to run 3 times a week until the race, some weeks I did that, and some weeks I fell short. Some weeks, I didn't run at all.

Starting out, my goal was to be able to complete the race in under an hour, and I was not sure at all if I was going to be able to do that. I started out running on the treadmill at my climbing gym on the days I wasn't climbing, I was using the Runna app for my training. The Runna app starts you off running for 1 minute, then walking for 90 seconds, then another minute, then more walking. You'll progress this way until you're running for a few minutes, then it switches you to distance based training. My problem in the beginning was that I was following the recommended speed according to the Runna app of 4.2 mph, if I remember correctly. This speed was WAY faster than I could handle, but I forced myself to push through it because I thought that that was what I needed to do if I wanted to become a runner.

After only the first few days, I was exhausted and in so much pain I wasn't able to complete even a single 90 second run at the speed I needed to be going. As with many struggles in my life, I consulted Reddit, and it was here that I learned that pretty much everybody in the r/Runna community agrees that you need to completely ignore the recommended speed and just run at whatever pace you feel comfortable at. They said that I needed to be going at a conversational pace, meaning that I could hold a conversation while I was running, and that I should feel like I'm going way slower than I'm meant to. After this, I started running at 3.5 mph and felt a little bit better, though I still couldn't imagine holding a conversation and, as I would soon discover, I was still running way faster than I should be.

I went out one morning to run on my gym's treadmill, and it was here that I learned that the gym didn't actually open until 10am on Mondays. Stressed but wanting to get a run in, I searched online for other options. I eventually settled on going to a track and running outside, I figured I probably wouldn't sweat too much since it was still a bit chilly and I would be okay to go to work after. Since it was still so cold out I also likely wouldn't have to worry about being seen by other people, my biggest fear with running outside. I went to the track and, unfortunately, it was covered in snow, so I wasn't able to run. Even though I couldn't actually run that day, it did inspire me to go back to the track next time the snow melted.

My first time running on the track was not that eventful, but it did mark the last time that I ever ran on a treadmill again. I went out with my coat because it was still chilly, even if it was not snowing, and I ran. I felt much better running on the track and I really enjoyed the fresh air as I ran. On future trips, I learned that Runna does not work very well when it is not connected to a watch for GPS. I dug out my old Galaxy Watch 4 and was disappointed to learn that Runna was not compatible with it, but I still wore it from then on to be able to track my heart rate.

My running plan was supposed to end the weekend of April 15th, and I was flying to DC for Mensa Mind Games 2026. My plan was to land in the early afternoon, take the train to the Smithsonian, train and bus back to the hotel, run a bit at the hotel gym, and go to bed. What actually happened was that my 7am flight was delayed until 10:15am, my second flight was delayed as well, and I didn't actually land until 6:30pm. I was so exhausted from waking up at 4am to take the bus to the airport that I didn't have any energy to run and told myself I would do it the next day after I get back from the Capitol. The next day wasn't much better, I had a great time at the Capitol but I walked over 25 thousand steps and again had absolutely no energy for running. My procrastination took over at that point, and I did not run again until Monday May 4th, less than a week before my race.

The week of the race was miserable, to say the least. After nearly a month without training, I ran a mile on that Monday before I had to stop because my legs hurt. On Wednesday, my goal was 2 miles, thankfully, I was able to run 2.5, but my legs HURT, again. I took two rest days before the race, where my goal was to run all 3.1 miles without stopping, or walking, one single time.

I woke up at 5am, my race started at 7am. I got dressed, played some Tears of the Kingdom, and got in my car to drive to my church where I would park and walk to the race. I got to the race at 6:20. The walk from the church was a good warm up, and my family and girlfriend came to see me before I started, and then, before I knew it, we were moving towards the starting line. This was it, the moment I've been training for. I was really going to do it.

As I crossed the starting line, I started running, if you can call it that. Throughout all of my training, and now at the race, I had been running barely faster than my walking speed, and by the time I get tired I'm usually going slower than my walking speed. That's okay, though, because the only goal for this race is to finish without walking or stopping. I see my family just past the start and they cheer me on. I know I'll see them again at the 2 mile mark, along with my sisters playing in the pep band. Not too long into the race, I'm faced with my first problem: I've never run on hills.

All of my training was completed on a track, perfectly flat ground designed for high schoolers to run 100 meter dashes and jump hurdles. The beginning of the course is on a downhill, which is nice, but it goes right back up about half a mile in. Going up that first hill was hard, really hard. My legs were hurting and I wanted to stop so badly. I was not ready for this, I hadn't trained for this, hell, I'd barely trained at all. What did I think I was doing? I'm not the kind of person who runs a 5k. I should just walk the hill and keep running once I get to the top.

I pushed away the thoughts and pushed myself to keep going. I was only a half mile in, and if I gave into the doubt now, I'd never finish this race. The next half mile is somewhat hilly, going up and down fairly frequently, but I can handle it. Thankfully, the next mile after that is at a consistent, though very slight, downhill. Unfortunately, that doesn't mean that it's easy. After passing the first mile marker there isn't much in the way of pep and cheering crowds, most of the group has gone way ahead of me, and I am pretty much alone, running at the same speed as the walkers around me.

My legs hurt, the doubts are constant, and I can barely hold on. Just before the two mile mark, there's a hill, and I can't stop thinking about how badly I want to stop running. Nearing the top of the hill, I see my dad. He’s alone, but I see him, and it gives me the strength to keep going. He explains that my sisters are apparently only at the two mile mark for the 10k and 25k. This is disappointing, but I don't let it get to me; I'm not going to stop. I see my girlfriend and step mom ahead, and my girlfriend looks as exhausted as me. She's not a morning person, but she woke up at 6am, a whole 3 hours earlier than she'd normally ever wake up, just to support me in my race. Seeing her gives me the energy to keep going.

Two and a half miles in, my leg starts cramping really bad and it hurts, but I push through. I talk for a small moment with the older woman walking next to me. She said she's been running the race for a few years now, but she's recovering from open heart surgery, and this year she just wants to finish. Despite the pain in my leg, I see the 3 mile marker and my energy comes back a little, so I push on and run a little bit faster. Turning a corner I see it: the finish line. I've run so long and I really shouldn't have any energy, but all of a sudden everything feels so real. The pain in my leg vanishes, and I run a bit faster. I knew from the beginning that I wanted to sprint through the finish line. A few minutes ago I had given up on that dream, I didn't have the energy, now, I wasn't tired at all.

In the last ~100 feet of the race, I just go. I sprint so hard and run through that finish line feeling amazing. I also feel a bit like I'm going to collapse. A medic congratulates me, silently checking for signs that I'm okay, and I walk on. I see my family, give some hugs, and collect my medal. I just ran a full 5k, no walking, no stopping. I did it.

It's been a few days now and my legs don't hurt anymore, though I could barely walk the day after the race. The race was hard, really hard, but it felt really great achieving a goal that I set for myself. I don't know if I'm going to run the race again next year. For now, I'm just proud of myself for doing it at all. I look forward to climbing again, I didn't mention it, but I haven't gone to the climbing gym since I started running outside. I just didn't have the energy with all the running I was doing. I'm not really sure how to end this blog post, but I guess my advice would be that it feels good to do something hard. I know that's pretty obvious, but I feel like, at times, I can feel hopeless, inadequate, just all around like a failure, but doing this race helped me believe in myself and feel more confident in my abilities. Even if I don't run again, I look forward to putting that same energy into everything else that I do. Thanks for reading!
